Vehicle Management System

Centralizing a manual fleet of thousands across a national hierarchy

Problem

One of the country's largest government fleets ran on paper and spreadsheets. No central visibility into availability, manual requisitions causing days of delay, unmonitored fuel misuse, and no real-time view of unit-wise surplus or shortage.

What I did

03

Plumber onboarding redesign

Fixing the step where half of all signups quit

Problem

The rewards app onboarded plumbers to track jobs and earn payouts, but nearly half abandoned at step three, where they had to enter personal info, upload ID, and add bank details on one long screen with no sense of progress.

What I did
  • Located the exact exit point with Firebase funnel analytics, then watched the hesitation directly through Hotjar session replays and heatmaps.
  • Interviewed dropped-off plumbers through field agents to learn the form felt long, opaque, and never-ending.
  • Split the step into two focused screens, added a progress bar, and rewrote labels in plain Bengali with tooltips explaining why each field was needed.
Outcome
60% → 28%step-three drop-off ↑ 22%onboarding completion higheractivation and reward-data reliability

Agile team turnaround & driver retention

Diagnosing a struggling team, and reversing rider churn

Challenge

A delivery team was underperforming: a passive tech lead, low morale, missed deadlines, buggy releases, and new stakeholders unfamiliar with Agile. Separately, a Pathao-style platform was losing drivers to competitors and needed a retention strategy grounded in real causes, not symptoms.

Approach
  • Mapped each team symptom (leadership, morale, teamwork, stakeholder ramp-up, quality) to a concrete intervention rather than one vague people problem.
  • Proposed an SME coaching loop for the tech lead with measurable KPIs and GROW-model goals, and tied missed deadlines to weak planning: INVEST stories, buffer estimation, CI/CD, and shift-left QA.
  • For driver churn, ran a prioritized root-cause analysis, chose income instability as the core problem, and designed three scored solutions: guaranteed minimum income, off-peak incentives, and an earnings-projection dashboard.
Deliverables

A full diagnostic and action plan for the team across leadership, morale, onboarding, collaboration, and delivery quality, plus a prioritized retention strategy with defined success metrics and a six-stage rollout from data analysis to nationwide launch.

What I've learned

01

Fall in love with the problem

Features are cheap to imagine and expensive to build. My best work came from spending longer understanding the problem than defending a solution.

02

Ship to learn

A plan is a hypothesis. Real progress comes from putting something small in front of users early and letting their behaviour, not opinions, decide what's next.

03

Adoption is the real launch

Going live is the start, not the finish line. The systems that mattered were the ones people actually used, so training, docs, and support earn a place on the roadmap.

04

Trust scales, control doesn't

Running five projects at once taught me to set clear outcomes and let capable people own the how. Micromanagement caps a team at the manager's bandwidth.

05

Data ends arguments

Opinions stall meetings; a number moves them. One honest metric or a simple dashboard resolves more debates than any deck.

06

Write it down

Most "miscommunication" is just unwritten assumptions. A crisp requirement, a stated non-goal, or a one-line acceptance criterion prevents a week of rework.

09

The Playbook

Free product & project education, the frameworks I lean on, written plainly for anyone moving into product. Pick a track, open a card. Short enough to read before an interview, concrete enough to use on Monday.

The problem space is what the user is struggling with; the solution space is what you build to help. Weak products jump straight to features. Strong product people stay in the problem long enough to understand it, then let the solution be the variable they change. The discipline is simple: separate "what's wrong" from "what we'll do," and never let a favourite feature define the problem backwards.

In practice

"Users churn at onboarding" is a problem. "Add a tutorial video" is one solution to it, and probably not the best one until you know why they churn.

Interview cue: "I fall in love with the problem, not the solution."

People don't buy products; they "hire" them to make progress in a situation. JTBD shifts focus from who the user is (demographics) to what they're trying to get done (the job) and the context around it. Define the job as a verb-led outcome, "help me track where my money goes", and competitors become anything else that could do that job, not just lookalike apps.

Example

The classic: people "hire" a morning milkshake to make a dull commute better and stay full till lunch. The real competitor is a banana, not another milkshake.

An output is what you ship, a feature, a release, tickets closed. An outcome is the change in user behaviour or business result it creates. Feature factories measure outputs and feel busy; good teams commit to outcomes and stay free to change the feature if it isn't moving the metric. Always ask: if we ship this, what will be measurably different, and for whom?

In practice

Output: "Launched referral page." Outcome: "Lifted activated users from invites by 12%." Only the second tells you if the work was worth doing.

Every idea rests on assumptions across four risks: desirability (do they want it?), viability (does it work for the business?), feasibility (can we build it?), and usability (can they use it?). Instead of building everything and hoping, list the assumptions, find the one that would sink the idea if false, and design the cheapest test for it first. De-risking beats predicting.

In practice

Before building a marketplace, the riskiest assumption is usually "sellers will list", test that with a landing page or manual outreach before writing code.

A map (from Teresa Torres) that keeps discovery honest. At the top sits one desired outcome. Beneath it branch the opportunities, unmet needs and pains from research. Under each sit candidate solutions, and under those the experiments that test them. It ties every idea back to a real need and a measurable outcome, instead of a flat wish-list.

Shape

Outcome → Opportunities → Solutions → Experiments. Read top-down to plan, bottom-up to justify.

The Lean loop: turn an idea into the smallest thing you can build, measure how real users respond, and learn whether to persevere or pivot. The goal of an early build isn't revenue, it's validated learning at the lowest cost. Speed through the loop matters more than polish on the first pass.

In practice

A concierge MVP, doing the service manually behind a simple form, can validate demand in a week without building the automation.

Strategy is as much what you won't do as what you will. Every yes spends a team's finite capacity and adds surface area to maintain forever. A good PM protects focus, weighing each request against the current outcome and declining the ones that don't serve it, kindly but clearly.

Frame

"That's a good idea, and it's not for this quarter" beats a backlog of half-built features nobody owns.

Prioritization is making your reasoning explicit so trade-offs can be debated. RICE scores each item by Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort, good for comparing many features. Value vs. Effort is the fast 2×2 for quick wins. Kano sorts features into basics (expected), performance (more is better), and delighters (unexpected joy). MoSCoW (Must/Should/Could/Won't) is best for scoping one release.

Rule of thumb

RICE to rank a backlog, MoSCoW to cut a release, Kano to balance the mix. No score decides for you, it structures the conversation.

A Product Requirements Document aligns everyone on what you're building and why, before design and engineering invest. A tight PRD covers: the problem and who has it, goals and non-goals (boundaries matter as much as scope), requirements / user stories, success metrics, and open questions. It describes the what and why, not the how.

Tell

If your PRD has no "non-goals" and no success metric, it's a feature spec, not a product decision.

A user story frames work from the user's view: "As a [user], I want [goal], so that [benefit]." Good stories follow INVEST, Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable. Acceptance criteria define "done," often written Given / When / Then, so QA and engineering share one definition of success.

Example

Given a logged-out user, When they enter valid credentials, Then they land on the dashboard within 2 seconds.

A North Star Metric is the single measure that best captures the value users get. AARRR ("pirate metrics") tracks the funnel: Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue. HEART (Google) measures experience: Happiness, Engagement, Adoption, Retention, Task success. Pair leading indicators (predict, e.g. activation) with lagging ones (confirm, e.g. revenue).

Watch for

Vanity metrics (total signups) feel good but don't guide decisions. Prefer ratios and retention over raw totals.

A roadmap communicates direction, not a delivery contract. Now / Next / Later replaces false-precision dates with confidence horizons: Now is committed and detailed, Next is shaped, Later is a theme. Organize around outcomes and themes ("reduce onboarding drop-off") rather than a feature list, so the team keeps freedom to find the best solution.

Why

Dated feature roadmaps become promises you break. Theme-based ones survive the inevitable learning.

Good teams run two tracks at once. Discovery continuously decides what's worth building, talking to users, prototyping, testing assumptions. Delivery builds and ships what discovery has validated. They feed each other: delivery frees capacity, discovery keeps the backlog full of de-risked, worth-doing work, so engineers aren't building on guesses.

Tell

If your backlog is full but no one has talked to a user in a month, discovery has stalled.

Building it isn't shipping it. A launch needs a plan: who the release is for, how they'll hear about it, what "ready" means (docs, support, enablement), the rollout shape (internal → beta → GA, or a phased %), and the metric that says it worked. Coordinating sales, support, and marketing is as much the PM's job as the spec.

Checklist

Audience · messaging · enablement · rollout plan · success metric · rollback path.

Waterfall is sequential (requirements, design, build, test, release) and works when scope is fixed and well understood (regulated, hardware, fixed-bid). Agile is iterative (small increments, frequent feedback, change welcomed) and fits when requirements evolve, which is most software. The honest answer is "it depends on uncertainty": the more you'll learn mid-flight, the more iterative you should be.

Tell

If "we'll know more after users try it" is true, don't lock a 9-month plan. Ship, learn, adjust.

Scrum delivers in fixed sprints (usually 1–2 weeks). Roles: Product Owner (owns the backlog), Scrum Master (removes blockers, guards the process), Developers (build the increment). Events: Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Review, Retrospective. Artifacts: Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Increment.

Common trap

The standup is for blockers and coordination, not a status report read to the manager.

Teams estimate in story points, relative size capturing effort, complexity, and uncertainty, rather than hours, because humans compare better than they predict. Planning Poker surfaces disagreement (a 2 vs. an 8 means you don't share understanding yet). Velocity is points completed per sprint, used to forecast capacity, never to compare teams or push people.

Mindset

Estimates are forecasts under uncertainty, not commitments. Re-estimate as you learn.

Risk management is continuous. Identify what could go wrong, assess each by probability × impact, then choose a response: avoid, mitigate, transfer, or accept. Track them in a living risk register with an owner and a trigger for each. Act on the few high-probability, high-impact risks early, while they're still cheap to handle.

Example

"Key vendor API may not be ready" → mitigate with a stub and a fallback path, owned by the tech lead, reviewed weekly.

Most project failures are communication failures. A RACI matrix makes roles explicit for each decision: Responsible (does the work), Accountable (one owner of the outcome), Consulted (gives input), Informed (kept in the loop). Pair it with a comms cadence matched to each stakeholder's influence and interest, so nobody's surprised and decisions don't stall.

Rule

Exactly one "A" per row. If two people are accountable, no one is.

The critical path is the longest chain of dependent tasks, it sets the earliest the project can finish, so a slip on any task on it slips the whole project. Map dependencies early, watch the critical path closely, and protect it. Work that isn't on the critical path has slack and can flex; work that is, can't.

In practice

If a vendor integration is on the critical path, start it first and give it a buffer, everything downstream waits on it.

Requirements will change, that's normal. Uncontrolled change is the problem. Change control means every new request is made visible, sized for its impact on scope, timeline, and budget, and explicitly accepted or deferred, not quietly absorbed. Saying "yes, and here's what moves" keeps trust intact and the plan honest.

Tell

"We can add it, that pushes launch two weeks, or we drop X." Make the trade-off visible; let the owner choose.
